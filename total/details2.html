Top Technology Habits of Highly Successful CEOs

Technology today is not only a part of our daily processes, it completely defines the way we live and work. As a CEO, there are two sides to the technology coin. On the one hand, it allows us to be connected to all aspects of our business without being physically tethered to our desks. This way, you can manage your business without micromanaging your people.

On the other hand, though, is the downside to connectivity: the ever-shrinking line between work and life. Not only are you unable to leave work at work, electronics can be productivity vampires.

To ensure technology helps more than hinders, check out these five healthy technology habits:

1. Schedule times to disconnect.When you’re available 24/7, you put yourself at risk for burnout. Instead, schedule two or three 15-minute breaks throughout your workday. Use those times to walk away from your computer, tablet, phone and any other connected devices. Get some fresh air, chat with an employee or read a few pages of a book. You’ll return refreshed, rejuvenated and ready to tackle your next task.

2. Lead by example. When you text during a meeting or stop a conversation with an employee to answer a phone call, you’re exercising bad etiquette and setting the stage for your entire company. As a leader, everyone looks to you as the model of appropriate behavior. Focus on facetime first. Unless it’s an absolute emergency, your phone call or text can wait.

3. Take advice from tech experts. Great business leaders know a streamlined technology environment is key to success. Sometimes all it takes is a little refining. A managed services partner can help you determine the best technology investments for your needs, without wasting a dime of your budget.

4. Don’t multitask. According to research by the University of Utah, 98 percent of people cannot multitask effectively. In other words, only 2 percent of humans can do more than one task as effectively, or more effectively, as one task alone. Chances are, you don’t fall into that 2 percent. Instead of checking email, talking on the phone and reviewing a report simultaneously, focus on one task at a time.
